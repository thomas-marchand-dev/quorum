SECTION 1

Why Medical Device Development Is Broken

Medical device development is one of the most expensive, slowest, and most documentation-intensive engineering processes in existence. A Class III device program requires 50–200 specialists over 5–12 years at a cost of $50–500M. The majority of that cost and time is not spent on innovation - it is spent on coordination, documentation, regulatory navigation, and quality system compliance.

The problem is structural, not intellectual. The engineers, designers, and clinicians who build medical devices are world-class. The regulatory framework exists for good reason - patient safety demands rigor. But the tools and workflows connecting these people to that framework have not evolved in 30 years.

1.1 The Capital Drought - Venture Funding Has Abandoned Early-Stage Medtech

The funding environment has made these structural problems existential. Medtech venture capital collapsed 57% from its 2021 peak of $22.5 billion to just $12.3 billion in 2023, according to PitchBook. While total deal value has partially recovered, deal volumes continue to fall - fewer companies are getting funded at all.

Early-stage funding has been hit hardest. Seed and Series A rounds fell 18% year-over-year in 2023. Capital is concentrating into later-stage, de-risked companies. Only companies with compelling AI/software components are seeing accelerated funding.

The math is devastating for device startups. Investors demand extensive clinical validation before writing a check. But clinical validation requires exactly the capital they won't deploy. Only 9 venture-backed medtech exits occurred in Q1 2025, far below historical norms.

1.2 The Pediatric Device Desert - Where the Problem Is Most Acute

The medtech industry's structural failures hit children hardest. 73 million children in the United States are routinely treated with devices designed, tested, and cleared for adults - squeezed into smaller bodies, used off-label, and monitored with algorithms trained on adult data. This is not a new problem. It has persisted for three decades despite repeated legislative and regulatory attempts to fix it, because the root cause is economic, not regulatory.

The Numbers

A landmark cohort study by Pathak, Bourgeois et al. (JAMA Pediatrics, 2022) examined every high-risk therapeutic device approved by the FDA from 2016 to 2021: of 124 Class III devices, only 2 (2%) were approved for children exclusively, and just 23 (19%) included any pediatric labeling at all. The remaining 80% were adults-only. Among those 25 pediatric devices, most were studied only in adult populations or with very small numbers of pediatric patients. Fewer than half had FDA-mandated post-approval studies enrolling children, and only one such study had been completed at time of publication.

The AI/ML device landscape is even worse. Brewster, Bourgeois et al. (JAMA Pediatrics, 2025) - from Boston Children's Hospital - reviewed 876 FDA-authorized AI/ML-enabled devices through March 2024: 83% had no pediatric label. Of the 17% that were labeled for children, only 19% reported developing their algorithms using pediatric datasets. The rest were validated only with adult data (15%) or failed to report whether children were studied at all (66%). Algorithms designed for adult anatomy, adult physiology, and adult disease processes are being applied to children without validation - a practice that would be unacceptable in pharmaceuticals but remains standard in devices.

The clinical consequences are documented. As Almond (Stanford/FDA CERSI, 2023) described: clinicians are forced to improvise - squeezing adult pacemakers into infants, using ventricular assist devices with outdated technology because the pediatric-specific version was never developed. Multiple studies confirm that children have inferior outcomes compared to adults when treated with devices designed for adult anatomy.

Espinoza et al. (Pediatrics, 2025) estimate that 60–75% of all medical products are used in pediatrics without prior clinical validation or the structured risk-benefit analysis that accompanies FDA clearance. The pediatric device clinical trial infrastructure at children's hospitals is fundamentally inadequate - drug trial infrastructure doesn't translate to device trials, and 32% of registered pediatric device studies between 2017 and 2022 were discontinued due to low patient accrual.

Why It Persists - The Economics

The problem is not ignorance or indifference. It is math. Developing a pediatric device adds 30–50% cost and time vs. an adult program for a fraction of the market. Pediatric populations are smaller, more heterogeneous, and harder to enroll. Regulatory pathways are more complex - no predicates for many pediatric indications, smaller clinical studies with less statistical power, and ethical constraints on studying children. The medtech business model offers 2–3× return on investment vs. 10× for drugs, with weaker IP protection - you can engineer around any device patent.

Congress has tried. The Pediatric Medical Device Safety and Improvement Act (2007) created the Pediatric Device Consortia (PDC) grant program. In 16 years, the PDCs have supported 19 devices to FDA clearance - an important contribution but a fraction of the need. The Humanitarian Device Exemption pathway has produced 29 approved devices since 2008, of which only 7 carry a pediatric indication. Legislative and regulatory tools alone cannot overcome a market failure this deep.

The result: a global pediatric device desert where children are particularly vulnerable to shortages, face fewer or no alternatives when devices are unavailable, and bear disproportionate risk from devices never designed for their bodies.

Not by cutting corners on safety or quality - but by eliminating the 30–40% of engineering time spent on documentation, the 15–20% spent in meetings, and the months of regulatory consulting that can be automated. Quorum exists to change the economics. By automating the most expensive and time-consuming parts of device development - regulatory strategy, quality documentation, risk management, clinical trial design, engineering coordination, and real-world evidence generation - Quorum reduces cost and time by 40–60% for Class II devices and 30–50% for Class III, making pediatric (and all) device development commercially rational for the first time.

2.3 Core Operating Principles

The OS Does Not Discriminate

Quorum treats every step of every program with the same rigor. The 200th design iteration receives the same thoroughness as the 1st. A routine 510(k) gets the same systematic analysis as a breakthrough PMA. The system is incapable of fatigue, boredom, or unconscious favoritism - eliminating the class of errors that cluster when human attention fades: late-stage documentation gaps, skipped edge cases, missed cross-references.

Thorough, but Never More Than Necessary

Quorum generates exactly what the FDA requires and nothing more. Every data element maps to a regulatory requirement. Every test protocol traces to a design input. Every risk row connects to a real hazard. The system does not pad submissions with boilerplate, over-test beyond what design inputs require, or generate documentation that serves no regulatory purpose. The OS is thorough because it never skips a step. It is efficient because it never adds one.

How Gate Reviews Work

One week before a scheduled gate, Quorum compiles and publishes a Gate Review Packet for every voter on that gate's review team. The packet is not a summary or a slide deck. It is the complete body of evidence the voter needs to make an informed GO or NO-GO decision, organized by their role:

  • The Lead Engineer's packet emphasizes design outputs, test results, and engineering trade-offs.
  • The RA Director's packet emphasizes regulatory alignment, predicate comparison, and submission readiness.
  • The QA Director's packet emphasizes DHF completeness, CAPA status, and process validation.
  • The Medical Director's packet emphasizes clinical rationale, risk-benefit, and patient safety.
  • The Venture Lead sees all of the above, plus the program-level timeline and budget impact.

Every packet is generated automatically from the live program data. No one writes a gate review presentation. No one assembles slides. The OS pulls from the DHF, the risk file, the RTM, the test reports, the regulatory strategy, and the budget model, and structures the information for each reviewer's domain.

Read Tracking: No Review, No Vote

Gate packets are delivered through the platform, which tracks reading progress at the section level. A reviewer cannot cast a vote until the platform confirms they have read the complete packet for their role. If a reviewer hasn't finished by the gate date, their vote is "pending" and the program does not advance. Reminders are sent at 5, 3, and 1 day before. The result: gate meetings are decision meetings, not presentations - every voter has already read the evidence. Meetings rarely exceed 60 minutes.

Gate Notes

G1 is the first gate where NO is common - roughly 40% of concepts do not pass. A NO here costs $5K in surgeon honoraria and 5 days of platform time; a NO after G3 costs $500K+. G3 is the most consequential engineering gate - after design freeze, any change requires a formal ECO (auto-drafted by QG​QualityGuard) and re-validation of affected protocols. G4 has the largest review team because it commits the program to regulatory filing. G5 includes the CEO because a submission carries the organization's reputation with the FDA. G6–G7 are Class III only. G6 involves the DSMB Chair for independent safety oversight; G7 is the broadest review team in the lifecycle.

Gate Principles

The OS enforces the sequence. No phase begins until the gate is passed. No gate is auto-approved. The system will not generate a V&V protocol until Design Freeze (G3) is passed, or compile an IDE until the Transfer Gate (G4) is passed. This prevents the most common failure mode: teams that start testing before the design is locked, or start writing submissions before testing is complete.

Every gate has the same structure. One week before, the OS publishes the complete evidence package. Each voter reviews independently (audio feedback is the default). On gate day, the H​Venture Lead presents the OS recommendation and the team votes: GO, CONDITIONAL GO, or NO-GO. The vote and all evidence are logged permanently.

4.6 Human Factors & Usability Engineering

Agents: DC DesignCopilot + RG RiskGuard + MP MarketPilot

IEC 62366 compliance - usability engineering for medical devices, use-related risk analysis, formative studies, and summative validation.

Current Industry Pain Points

  • Human factors is treated as a regulatory checkbox. Companies commission a summative usability study 3 months before submission, discover critical use errors, and redesign under time pressure. FDA increasingly rejects submissions with inadequate human factors evidence - it is now the #2 reason for 510(k) Additional Information requests.
  • Use-related risk analysis is disconnected from the DFMEA. Engineering risk management (ISO 14971) and usability risk analysis (IEC 62366) are maintained as separate documents by separate teams. Use errors that could trigger device hazards are missed because the two risk files never talk to each other.
  • Formative studies are skipped or informal. Early-stage usability feedback is gathered through hallway conversations, not structured evaluations. By the time formal testing reveals use errors, the design is frozen and changes are expensive.
  • Pediatric use environments are unique. Children's hospital ORs are smaller, surgical teams work with scaled-down anatomy, caregivers (parents) are part of the use environment for home-use devices, and the user population ranges from neonatal nurses to adolescent patients managing their own care. Generic adult usability frameworks miss these factors entirely.

Quorum Capabilities

Integrated Use-Risk Analysis

  • Unified risk model: RG​RiskGuard maintains a single risk file that integrates both device failure modes (ISO 14971 DFMEA) and use-related failure modes (IEC 62366 use-risk analysis). Every use step in the device workflow is mapped to the hazards it could trigger. When the design changes, both risk dimensions update simultaneously.
  • Use error prediction from surgeon interviews: The 10 surgeon interviews from market validation (Section 3) are analyzed for use-related signals - hand size constraints, grip preferences, visual access limitations, workflow sequence expectations. MP​MarketPilot extracts usability insights from the same audio data used for market validation, feeding them into the design process as use-related design inputs.
  • Pediatric use environment modeling: The OS maintains use environment profiles for pediatric settings - OR dimensions at children's hospitals, lighting, staffing configurations (surgeon + 1-2 assistants vs. adult OR teams of 4-6), patient positioning constraints for small anatomy, and caregiver involvement models for home-use devices.

Formative Usability Through the Build Cycle

  • Continuous formative feedback: Every time a clinician interacts with a prototype during the design-build-test cycle (Section 4.7), DC​DesignCopilot captures usability data from the audio narration - fumbles, hesitations, workarounds, verbal confusion. These are coded as formative usability observations and linked to specific design features.
  • Task analysis auto-generation: From the device's intended use and the IFU, the OS generates a complete task analysis - every step the user performs, the knowledge required, the physical actions, the feedback the device provides, and the potential error modes at each step. This task analysis is the backbone of both the formative and summative study protocols.
  • Formative study protocol generation: TD​TrialDesigner generates structured formative evaluation protocols from the task analysis - specifying tasks, scenarios, participant profiles, data collection methods, and success criteria. Studies run during the prototype phase, not after freeze.

Summative Validation

  • Summative study design: The summative (validation) usability study protocol is generated from the cumulative formative data - focusing on the critical tasks and use scenarios where errors were observed or predicted. No wasted test time on low-risk interactions that formative data already confirmed are safe.
  • Participant recruitment matching: For pediatric devices, the OS specifies the exact user profiles needed - pediatric orthopedic surgeons (SmartCast), pediatric cardiologists and perfusionists (heart valve), parents/caregivers (home-use devices) - with minimum experience levels and representativeness criteria.
  • Automated usability study report: DW​DocuWriter compiles the human factors report from formative and summative data - task analysis, use-risk analysis, formative study results, summative validation results, and residual use-risk assessment - in the format FDA expects for 510(k) or PMA submission.

Human Oversight Model

  • H​Lead Engineer reviews all use-related design changes and validates that mitigations address the identified use errors.
  • H​Medical Director approves summative study protocol and reviews critical use-error findings for clinical significance.
  • H​RA Director validates that the human factors evidence package meets FDA guidance (FDA HFE guidance) before submission.

Value to the Venture Studio

Traditional human factors program: $150K-$300K for a dedicated HFE consultant, 4-6 months of standalone formative and summative studies, discovered too late to influence design. Quorum: usability data captured continuously during the build cycle (zero incremental cost), use-risk analysis integrated with the device DFMEA (one risk file, not two), formative observations coded from prototype sessions that are already happening, and summative studies focused only on tasks where formative data showed risk. By the time the summative study runs, the design has already been refined through 3+ rounds of real-world usability feedback.

4.7 Sterilization & Packaging Validation

Agents: QG QualityGuard + DC DesignCopilot + RN RegNavigator

ISO 11135 (EtO), ISO 11137 (radiation), ISO 11607 (packaging), and ASTM D4169 (transport simulation) compliance - the complete sterile barrier system from process validation through shelf-life demonstration.

Current Industry Pain Points

  • Sterilization validation is a 4-6 month serial bottleneck. Bioburden testing, sterilization dose establishment (for radiation) or cycle development (for EtO), sterility assurance level (SAL) demonstration, and residual analysis run sequentially. Each step depends on the previous result. A failed bioburden triggers re-testing that delays the entire chain.
  • EtO residual limits are tightening. FDA and EPA are increasing scrutiny on ethylene oxide residuals and emissions. Companies that haven't planned for lower residual targets discover at validation that their cycle parameters need re-development - adding 2-3 months.
  • Packaging validation is underestimated. Seal strength testing, dye penetration, accelerated aging (per ASTM F1980), and transport simulation are treated as afterthoughts. Packaging failures discovered during stability testing force redesign of the sterile barrier system after the device design is frozen.
  • Pediatric devices have unique packaging challenges. Smaller devices require smaller packages, but sterile barrier integrity must be maintained. Size-specific packaging for multiple device sizes (e.g., SmartCast in 5 pediatric sizes) multiplies the validation matrix.
  • Shelf-life claims require 12-18 months of real-time aging data unless accelerated aging is accepted - and accelerated aging protocols must be validated before real-time data can be waived.

Quorum Capabilities

Sterilization Process Design

  • Method selection from material and device profile: DC​DesignCopilot evaluates sterilization compatibility during material selection - flagging materials that degrade under radiation (dose limits), that absorb EtO (residual risk), or that are incompatible with steam. The sterilization method is selected during design, not discovered during validation.
  • Bioburden-informed cycle development: QG​QualityGuard designs the sterilization validation protocol from the device's bioburden data and material compatibility profile. For EtO: cycle parameters (temperature, humidity, gas concentration, exposure time, aeration) optimized to achieve SAL 10⁻⁶ while minimizing residuals. For radiation: dose mapping and dose audit protocols generated from device geometry and density.
  • EtO residual planning: For devices sterilized with ethylene oxide, the OS calculates expected residual levels (EO, ECH) per ISO 10993-7 based on material absorption characteristics and aeration parameters - before the first validation run. Residual limits adjusted for pediatric body weight ranges (same approach as biocompatibility thresholds in Section 5.4).

Packaging System Design & Validation

  • Sterile barrier system specification: DC​DesignCopilot specifies packaging materials, seal parameters, and Tyvek/film configurations matched to the device geometry, sterilization method, and distribution requirements. For multi-size devices, the OS generates the full packaging matrix with shared materials and size-specific dimensions.
  • Validation protocol suite: QG​QualityGuard generates the complete packaging validation protocol suite: seal strength (ASTM F88), burst testing (ASTM F2095), dye penetration (ASTM F1929), whole package integrity, accelerated aging (ASTM F1980 with Q10=2 factor), and distribution simulation (ASTM D4169).
  • Accelerated aging calculation: The OS calculates accelerated aging equivalence from the claimed shelf life, storage conditions, and test temperature - generating the test duration and acceptance criteria. Real-time aging runs in parallel; accelerated aging data supports initial submission.

Validation Execution & Integration

  • Vendor coordination: Sterilization vendor (contract sterilizer) and packaging supplier milestones integrated into TP​TaskPilot's project schedule. Bioburden testing, dose audit, and sterility testing sequenced for minimum total timeline with dependencies mapped.
  • Cross-program sterilization data: When multiple programs use the same sterilization vendor and method (e.g., EtO at SteriGenics), cycle development data from the first program informs the second. Bioburden profiles for similar materials carry forward. The studio's 5th EtO-sterilized device validates faster than the 1st.
  • Submission-ready documentation: DW​DocuWriter compiles sterilization and packaging validation reports as test results arrive - bioburden report, sterilization validation report, packaging validation report, and stability protocol/data - formatted for the applicable 510(k) or PMA section.

Human Oversight Model

  • H​QA Director approves sterilization validation protocols and reviews all test results before report release.
  • H​Lead Engineer validates packaging design specifications and seal parameter selections.
  • H​RA Director confirms sterilization and packaging evidence meets FDA submission requirements for the applicable pathway.

Value to the Venture Studio

Traditional sterilization and packaging validation: 4-6 months sequential, $100K-$250K per device, frequently reworked when material or packaging changes occur late. Quorum: sterilization method selected during material selection (not after freeze), EtO residuals modeled before validation (no surprises), packaging matrix optimized across device sizes, accelerated aging initiated at design freeze to run in parallel with V&V. Cross-program sterilization data (same vendor, same method) reduces cycle development time for subsequent programs. Total timeline compressed from 4-6 months to 8-12 weeks through parallel execution and upfront planning.

4.8 Software, Firmware & Cybersecurity

Current Industry Pain Points

  • Software is the fastest-growing cause of FDA recalls. Software defects accounted for 34% of all Class I recalls in 2023. Most defects trace to inadequate requirements, not coding errors - the software did exactly what it was told to do, but what it was told to do was wrong.
  • IEC 62304 compliance is bolted on after development. Software teams build first, then retrofit lifecycle documentation - software requirements, architecture, unit tests, integration tests, and traceability. The documentation describes what was built, not what was planned. FDA reviewers see the gap immediately.

Most companies discover these requirements 6 months before filing.

The heart valve delivery system has deployment control software. Each of these triggers IEC 62304, and potentially SaMD classification, with device-class-level regulatory requirements.

When code changes, the architecture document updates. There is no gap between documented design and implemented code.

  • Threat modeling at concept: RG​RiskGuard generates a cybersecurity threat model (per FDA guidance) from the device's connectivity profile - network interfaces, wireless protocols, cloud connections, update mechanisms, and data storage. Threat categories (STRIDE model): spoofing, tampering, repudiation, information disclosure, denial of service, elevation of privilege.

When a CVE is published affecting a component in the SBOM, the alert reaches the Pod Lead with severity assessment and remediation options within hours.
  • Patch and update architecture: For connected devices, DC​DesignCopilot designs the secure update mechanism as a design input from Day 1 - not retrofitted before submission. The update pathway, authentication, rollback capability, and post-update verification are documented in the cybersecurity management plan.
  • Vulnerability management plan: RN​RegNavigator generates the post-market cybersecurity management plan required by FDA - defining how vulnerabilities will be monitored, assessed, communicated, and patched throughout the device's lifecycle.

SaMD / SiMD Classification

  • Regulatory pathway determination: For software-driven functionality (SmartCast's sizing algorithm, for example), RN​RegNavigator determines whether the software is Software as a Medical Device (SaMD), Software in a Medical Device (SiMD), or a non-device accessory - and the regulatory implications of each classification.
  • Algorithm validation for pediatric populations: When software includes clinical algorithms (e.g., sizing based on anatomical measurements), TD​TrialDesigner generates the algorithm validation protocol with pediatric-specific datasets - ensuring the algorithm is validated across the full pediatric size range, not just adult anatomy extrapolated downward.

4.9 Environmental & Electrical Safety Testing

Agents: DC DesignCopilot + QG QualityGuard + RN RegNavigator

IEC 60601-1 (electrical safety), IEC 60601-1-2 (EMC), ASTM F2052 (MRI safety), and environmental conditioning standards - the full suite of safety and compatibility testing required before any powered or implantable device can be submitted.

Current Industry Pain Points

  • Test lab scheduling is a 3-6 month lead time. Accredited EMC and electrical safety labs (UL, TUV, Intertek) have limited capacity. Companies that don't book slots 6 months in advance discover at V&V planning that testing won't start for another quarter.
  • Test failures at the lab are catastrophically expensive. An EMC failure (radiated emissions, conducted susceptibility) discovered during formal testing requires a hardware redesign - new PCB layout, shielding, filtering - followed by re-testing at $30K-$80K per re-run. The root cause is usually inadequate EMC design review during development.
  • MRI safety classification is left to the last minute. For implantable devices (growth rod, heart valve), MRI compatibility (MR Safe, MR Conditional, MR Unsafe) determines whether patients can ever receive an MRI scan after implantation. Testing is specialized, expensive ($50K-$150K), and the classification has profound clinical implications that should inform design from the start.
  • Environmental testing requirements vary by device type. Temperature cycling, humidity exposure, vibration, mechanical shock, drop testing, ingress protection (IP rating) - the applicable test suite depends on the device's intended use environment, transport conditions, and storage requirements. Companies frequently discover missing tests during FDA review.

The test matrix is known before the first prototype, not discovered during submission prep.

EMC risk assessment performed on the schematic before PCB layout, when fixes are cheap. By the time the device reaches the test lab, the design has been pre-screened for the most common failure modes.
  • MRI safety strategy at concept: For implantable devices, the MRI compatibility classification is determined during material selection and mechanical design. DC​DesignCopilot flags ferromagnetic materials, evaluates RF heating risk from conductive geometries, and recommends design modifications for MR Conditional labeling - before a single test is run.

Test Lab Coordination

  • Lab booking integrated into project schedule: TP​TaskPilot books accredited lab slots 4-6 months ahead based on the program's gate dates. When a design change pushes the V&V timeline, the OS automatically evaluates lab rescheduling options and notifies the Venture Lead with cost and timeline impact.

Protocols arrive at the lab complete and lab-compatible - no 2-week back-and-forth on protocol interpretation.
  • Environmental test matrix: Temperature, humidity, vibration, shock, and drop test profiles generated from the device's intended use environment (OR, home, ambulance), storage conditions, and transport packaging. For SmartCast: IP67 water ingress (waterproof claim), drop from 1.5m (child use), temperature cycling -20°C to +50°C (shipping). Each test linked to a design input and a labeling claim.

A test failure triggers an immediate impact assessment: which design inputs are affected, what redesign options exist, and what is the schedule impact.

The labeling is traceable to specific test results, not written by a regulatory consultant from memory.

2.3 AI/ML Architecture

2.3.1 Foundation Model Strategy. Hybrid RAG + selective fine-tuning. Model-agnostic abstraction layer allows swapping underlying models as capabilities improve. No vendor lock-in.

2.3.2 Training Data Architecture. Three tiers:

  • Tier 1 - Regulatory & Standards (public): FDA guidance, 510(k)/PMA/De Novo database (37K+ records), MAUDE pediatric subset (~85K), ISO standards, EU MDR, Harvard-MIT CRS. ~4.2M documents, ~18B tokens.
  • Tier 2 - Institutional (BCH-restricted, de-identified): BCH CHIP EHR (30 years), surgical outcomes databases, clinical trial templates, prior FDA submissions. Governed by BCH IRB.
  • Tier 3 - Dynamic (real-time): FDA Federal Register (24-hour pipeline), new MAUDE reports (weekly), ISO revisions, PubMed monitoring, PCX updates.
  • Tier 4 - Studio Observational (new, unique to this approach): Real workflows observed from the live studio - design review transcripts, test report drafts, regulatory strategy documents, engineering decision rationale. This tier is what makes Quorum different from any academic AI project: it learns from practitioners doing real work, not from published literature alone.

2.3.3 Agent Orchestration. TaskPilot coordinates all agents via: DAG-based task graph engine (Temporal/Prefect), structured JSON-LD inter-agent communication, and Human-in-the-Loop gates (HOL-1 informational, HOL-2 review required, HOL-3 signature required per 21 CFR Part 11). There is no HOL-0 - no agent output is invisible to human oversight. Inter-agent conflicts are escalated to humans, never resolved autonomously.

2.3.4 Hallucination Mitigation. Four-layer validation: source grounding with inline citations, cross-agent verification for high-stakes outputs, automated consistency checks against regulatory databases, and human expert review for all HOL-2/3 outputs.

2.3.5 PRR-Bench. The Pediatric Regulatory Reasoning Benchmark - a published, standardized evaluation framework:

  • 50 historical pediatric 510(k) submissions with known outcomes
  • 25 De Novo and PMA submissions with pediatric indications
  • 100 pediatric MAUDE scenarios requiring risk classification
  • 30 clinical trial design scenarios requiring pediatric-specific methodology
  • Targets: pathway accuracy >95%; predicate precision/recall >90%/>85%; document quality ≥3.8/5.0 (scored by ≥3 blinded former CDRH reviewers, Cohen's kappa >0.75)

2.4 Platform Validation & FDA Compliance

Quorum is not a medical device - it does not diagnose or treat patients. But its QMS functions (electronic records, electronic signatures, DHF management, CAPA tracking, design history, supplier qualification) must be validated software before any studio program can rely on it for quality records that FDA will audit. This is the same regulatory bar that commercial eQMS platforms (Greenlight Guru, MasterControl, Veeva Vault QMS) must clear. Without it, the platform is a research prototype, not a deployable tool.

2.4.1 Regulatory Framework. Quorum's QMS modules must comply with:

  • 21 CFR Part 11: Electronic records and electronic signatures. Audit trails, access controls, signature manifestations, system validation documentation. Every quality record generated through Quorum must be legally equivalent to a signed paper record.
  • 21 CFR 820 (Quality System Regulation): The QMS functionality (document control, CAPA, DHF, design controls, supplier management) must produce records that satisfy FDA QSR requirements during facility audits.
  • ISO 13485:2016 §4.1.6: Validation of computer software used in the quality management system. Risk-based approach per GAMP 5 (Good Automated Manufacturing Practice).
  • EU MDR Annex IX: For future EU market access, the QMS must meet the notified body audit requirements for computerized systems.

2.4.2 Validation Approach. Computer System Validation (CSV) following GAMP 5 risk-based methodology:

  • System classification: Quorum's QMS modules are GAMP Category 5 (custom software). AI agent outputs that feed into quality records require additional validation specific to non-deterministic systems - a novel challenge the program will address and publish.
  • Validation deliverables: Validation Master Plan, User Requirements Specification (URS), Functional Requirements Specification (FRS), Design Specification (DS), Installation Qualification (IQ), Operational Qualification (OQ), Performance Qualification (PQ), Traceability Matrix, Validation Summary Report.
  • AI-specific validation: Standard CSV assumes deterministic software (same input → same output). Quorum's agents are non-deterministic. The program will develop and publish a validation framework for AI-assisted QMS functions, including: output consistency testing (statistical bounds on agent variation), accuracy benchmarking (PRR-Bench integration), drift detection (ongoing monitoring of agent output quality), and human-override documentation (every agent output that a human modifies is logged as a training signal).
  • Part 11 technical controls: Immutable audit trail (every record creation, modification, and signature logged with timestamp, user identity, and reason for change), role-based access control, electronic signature with two-factor authentication, system backup and disaster recovery, automatic session timeout.

2.4.3 Validation Timeline. CSV is not a one-time event - it phases with the platform development:

  • Phase 1 (Mo 1-18): Validation Master Plan authored. URS and FRS developed in parallel with agent development. Part 11 technical controls implemented in platform architecture from Day 1 (not retrofitted).
  • Phase 2 (Mo 18-30): IQ/OQ executed on QMS modules (document control, CAPA, DHF, electronic signatures). QualityGuard agent validated against manual QMS outputs from studio's traditional operations. Gap analysis vs. Greenlight Guru / MasterControl feature parity.
  • Phase 3 (Mo 30-42): PQ executed on live studio program. AI-specific validation framework published (novel contribution). Full Part 11 compliance audit by independent assessor. Platform cleared for production use on quality records.
  • Phase 4 (Mo 42-54): Continuous validation - ongoing monitoring of agent output quality, periodic re-qualification, change control for model updates. FDA establishment registration if required for QMS software distribution.

Novel Contribution: Validating Non-Deterministic AI in QMS

No published framework exists for validating AI/ML-powered quality management systems under 21 CFR Part 11 and ISO 13485. Current GAMP 5 guidance assumes deterministic software. Quorum's validation work will produce the first peer-reviewed methodology for CSV of agentic AI in regulated medical device quality systems - a contribution that benefits the entire industry, not just this program. This methodology will be published and included in the open-source release.
