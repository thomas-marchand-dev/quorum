SECTION 1

Why Medical Device Development Is Broken

Medical device development is one of the most expensive, slowest, and most documentation-intensive engineering processes in existence. A Class III device program requires 50–200 specialists over 5–12 years at a cost of $50–500M. The majority of that cost and time is not spent on innovation - it is spent on coordination, documentation, regulatory navigation, and quality system compliance.

The problem is structural, not intellectual. The engineers, designers, and clinicians who build medical devices are world-class. The regulatory framework exists for good reason - patient safety demands rigor. But the tools and workflows connecting these people to that framework have not evolved in 30 years.

1.1 The Capital Drought - Venture Funding Has Abandoned Early-Stage Medtech

The funding environment has made these structural problems existential. Medtech venture capital collapsed 57% from its 2021 peak of $22.5 billion to just $12.3 billion in 2023, according to PitchBook. While total deal value has partially recovered, deal volumes continue to fall - fewer companies are getting funded at all.

Early-stage funding has been hit hardest. Seed and Series A rounds fell 18% year-over-year in 2023. Capital is concentrating into later-stage, de-risked companies. Only companies with compelling AI/software components are seeing accelerated funding.

The math is devastating for device startups. Investors demand extensive clinical validation before writing a check. But clinical validation requires exactly the capital they won't deploy. Only 9 venture-backed medtech exits occurred in Q1 2025, far below historical norms.

1.2 The Pediatric Device Desert - Where the Problem Is Most Acute

The medtech industry's structural failures hit children hardest. 73 million children in the United States are routinely treated with devices designed, tested, and cleared for adults - squeezed into smaller bodies, used off-label, and monitored with algorithms trained on adult data. This is not a new problem. It has persisted for three decades despite repeated legislative and regulatory attempts to fix it, because the root cause is economic, not regulatory.

The Numbers

A landmark cohort study by Pathak, Bourgeois et al. (JAMA Pediatrics, 2022) examined every high-risk therapeutic device approved by the FDA from 2016 to 2021: of 124 Class III devices, only 2 (2%) were approved for children exclusively, and just 23 (19%) included any pediatric labeling at all. The remaining 80% were adults-only. Among those 25 pediatric devices, most were studied only in adult populations or with very small numbers of pediatric patients. Fewer than half had FDA-mandated post-approval studies enrolling children, and only one such study had been completed at time of publication.

The AI/ML device landscape is even worse. Brewster, Bourgeois et al. (JAMA Pediatrics, 2025) - from Boston Children's Hospital - reviewed 876 FDA-authorized AI/ML-enabled devices through March 2024: 83% had no pediatric label. Of the 17% that were labeled for children, only 19% reported developing their algorithms using pediatric datasets. The rest were validated only with adult data (15%) or failed to report whether children were studied at all (66%). Algorithms designed for adult anatomy, adult physiology, and adult disease processes are being applied to children without validation - a practice that would be unacceptable in pharmaceuticals but remains standard in devices.

The clinical consequences are documented. As Almond (Stanford/FDA CERSI, 2023) described: clinicians are forced to improvise - squeezing adult pacemakers into infants, using ventricular assist devices with outdated technology because the pediatric-specific version was never developed. Multiple studies confirm that children have inferior outcomes compared to adults when treated with devices designed for adult anatomy.

Espinoza et al. (Pediatrics, 2025) estimate that 60–75% of all medical products are used in pediatrics without prior clinical validation or the structured risk-benefit analysis that accompanies FDA clearance. The pediatric device clinical trial infrastructure at children's hospitals is fundamentally inadequate - drug trial infrastructure doesn't translate to device trials, and 32% of registered pediatric device studies between 2017 and 2022 were discontinued due to low patient accrual.

Why It Persists - The Economics

The problem is not ignorance or indifference. It is math. Developing a pediatric device adds 30–50% cost and time vs. an adult program for a fraction of the market. Pediatric populations are smaller, more heterogeneous, and harder to enroll. Regulatory pathways are more complex - no predicates for many pediatric indications, smaller clinical studies with less statistical power, and ethical constraints on studying children. The medtech business model offers 2–3× return on investment vs. 10× for drugs, with weaker IP protection - you can engineer around any device patent.

Congress has tried. The Pediatric Medical Device Safety and Improvement Act (2007) created the Pediatric Device Consortia (PDC) grant program. In 16 years, the PDCs have supported 19 devices to FDA clearance - an important contribution but a fraction of the need. The Humanitarian Device Exemption pathway has produced 29 approved devices since 2008, of which only 7 carry a pediatric indication. Legislative and regulatory tools alone cannot overcome a market failure this deep.

The result: a global pediatric device desert where children are particularly vulnerable to shortages, face fewer or no alternatives when devices are unavailable, and bear disproportionate risk from devices never designed for their bodies.

The Core Problem Quorum Solves

If it costs $50–500M and 5–12 years to develop a medical device, and the pediatric market is too small to justify that investment at traditional economics, then the only solution is to structurally reduce the cost and time of development itself. Not by cutting corners on safety or quality — but by automating the work that consumes the most senior talent for the longest time.

The single largest drag on medtech productivity is requirements interpretation: engineers, quality specialists, and regulatory professionals spending weeks reading hundreds of pages of FDA guidance documents, ISO standards, and predicate device histories for their specific product class — then digesting them, cross-correlating requirements to unmet clinical needs, and manually mapping them into design inputs, test protocols, and submission sections. This interpretive work is repeated for every new program, by every new team, from scratch. It is skilled, expensive, and almost entirely automatable.

Quorum automates this entire chain — from regulatory strategy and requirements cross-correlation, through quality documentation and risk management, to clinical trial design, engineering coordination, and real-world evidence generation. The platform reduces development cost and time by 40–60% for Class II devices and 30–50% for Class III, making pediatric (and all) device development commercially rational for the first time.

Why "Quorum"?

The name reflects a core design principle: the agents do the work, but humans form the quorum that validates it. In every governance model, a quorum is the minimum number of qualified decision-makers required to authorize an action. In Quorum the platform, AI agents draft, analyze, generate, and coordinate — but no output moves forward without a quorum of human experts signing off. A gate review requires a quorum of voters. A design change requires a quorum of the Lead Engineer, QA Director, and Manufacturing Tech. A regulatory submission requires a quorum of the RA Director, Medical Director, and Venture Lead. The platform does not replace human judgment — it ensures that every human decision is made with complete information, full traceability, and zero administrative overhead. The AI handles the volume. The quorum ensures the quality.

SECTION 2

Quorum - Platform Architecture

A hierarchical, coordinated system of specialized agents - each trained on domain-specific medtech knowledge - that operate under TaskPilot and surface only the decisions requiring human expert judgment.

The problems described in Section 1 are not mysteries. Everyone in medtech knows that development takes too long, costs too much, drowns in documentation, and starves the patients who need devices most. The pediatric case is the sharpest version of this failure: small markets, complex anatomy, absent predicates, and a regulatory system designed for adult devices applied to children. These are common-sense problems with known causes - and yet nothing has changed in 30 years.

The Pediatric Venture Studio, affiliated with Boston Children's Hospital, attacks the structural problem from two directions simultaneously. The studio model (Section 11) creates the organizational engine: mutualized experts, dedicated Pods, shared infrastructure, and a portfolio approach that spreads fixed costs across programs - with BCH and philanthropic capital funding approximately 70% of development, removing the dependency on venture investors who have abandoned the space. But the studio model alone is not enough. A lean organization running traditional workflows still burns through philanthropic capital at $15M–$30M per Class II program - limiting the portfolio to a handful of devices and exhausting donor patience before the model proves itself.

The AI layer - Quorum - is what makes the economics work. The platform alone reduces development cost by 40–60% for Class II and 30–50% for Class III through automation of requirements interpretation, documentation, coordination, and compliance. Combined with the studio model's structural advantages — mutualized experts, shared infrastructure, portfolio economics, and BCH's 70% capital contribution — the total effect transforms a $15M pediatric device program into a $2M–$4M program (73–87% total reduction). At that cost structure, pediatric devices become commercially rational. Strategics can justify a Build-to-Buy (Section 12). Investors can underwrite the risk (Section 10). And the 83% of FDA-cleared devices that currently have no pediatric label become a market opportunity instead of a humanitarian gap.

Without efficiency, pediatric devices will never be attractive to commercialization partners. Quorum exists to create that efficiency.

Three-Tier Architecture

Tier 1 - TP​TaskPilot: Manages the complete development workflow for all active programs; tracks every program from concept through post-market surveillance; assigns tasks to specialist agents; maintains the Integrated Project Schedule in real time; routes decisions requiring human sign-off with full context pre-loaded.

Tier 2 - Domain Specialist Agents (12 agents): Each performs expert-level work in its domain - regulatory, clinical, quality, risk, engineering design, supply chain, real-world evidence, documentation, intellectual property, financial intelligence, go-to-market, and studio operations - trained on domain-specific corpora.

Tier 3 - Tool-Calling Workers: Sub-agents that execute specific tasks (database queries, document assembly, statistical calculation, CAD file parsing, bench test data ingestion). All outputs are reviewed by human domain experts before regulatory or clinical use.

Voice-First Interface

Quorum is voice-first. Every team member — engineers, QA directors, medical directors, venture leads — wears professional headphones with a microphone. They interact with the OS by speaking: giving feedback, narrating tests, approving documents, logging decisions, responding to review cards. Everything is transcribed instantly and filed into the correct record. There is no typing, no form-filling, no toggling between applications.

Voice interaction works everywhere — at the bench, in the lab, on a phone while commuting, or at home. A detailed review that would take 15 minutes to type takes less than a minute to speak. After transcription, the reviewer can edit the text if needed, but the default is speak-and-file. The result: dramatically faster feedback loops, higher participation in review cycles, and zero documentation overhead.

Voice signatures: Quality documents, ECOs, gate reviews, and other controlled records can be signed by voice command. The OS authenticates the signer, timestamps the signature, and logs it as a Part 11–compliant electronic signature. At the end of each day, engineers and reviewers are prompted to voice-sign any pending documents — a 2-minute daily routine that replaces hours of weekly paperwork.

2.3 Core Operating Principles

The OS Does Not Discriminate

Quorum treats every step of every program with the same rigor. The 200th design iteration receives the same thoroughness as the 1st. A routine 510(k) gets the same systematic analysis as a breakthrough PMA. The system is incapable of fatigue, boredom, or unconscious favoritism - eliminating the class of errors that cluster when human attention fades: late-stage documentation gaps, skipped edge cases, missed cross-references.

Thorough, but Never More Than Necessary

Quorum generates exactly what the FDA requires and nothing more. Every data element maps to a regulatory requirement. Every test protocol traces to a design input. Every risk row connects to a real hazard. The system does not pad submissions with boilerplate, over-test beyond what design inputs require, or generate documentation that serves no regulatory purpose. The OS is thorough because it never skips a step. It is efficient because it never adds one.

How Gate Reviews Work

One week before a scheduled gate, Quorum compiles and publishes a Gate Review Packet for every voter on that gate's review team. The packet is not a summary or a slide deck. It is the complete body of evidence the voter needs to make an informed GO or NO-GO decision, organized by their role:

  • The Lead Engineer's packet emphasizes design outputs, test results, and engineering trade-offs.
  • The RA Director's packet emphasizes regulatory alignment, predicate comparison, and submission readiness.
  • The QA Director's packet emphasizes DHF completeness, CAPA status, and process validation.
  • The Medical Director's packet emphasizes clinical rationale, risk-benefit, and patient safety.
  • The Venture Lead sees all of the above, plus the program-level timeline and budget impact.

Every packet is generated automatically from the live program data. No one writes a gate review presentation. No one assembles slides. The OS pulls from the DHF, the risk file, the RTM, the test reports, the regulatory strategy, and the budget model, and structures the information for each reviewer's domain.

Read Tracking: No Review, No Vote

Gate packets are delivered through the platform, which tracks reading progress at the section level. A reviewer cannot cast a vote until the platform confirms they have read the complete packet for their role. If a reviewer hasn't finished by the gate date, their vote is "pending" and the program does not advance. Reminders are sent at 5, 3, and 1 day before. The result: gate meetings are decision meetings, not presentations - every voter has already read the evidence. Meetings rarely exceed 60 minutes.

Gate Notes

G1 is the first gate where NO is common - roughly 40% of concepts do not pass. A NO here costs $5K in surgeon honoraria and 5 days of platform time; a NO after G3 costs $500K+. G3 is the most consequential engineering gate - after design freeze, any change requires a formal ECO (auto-drafted by QG​QualityGuard) and re-validation of affected protocols. G4 has the largest review team because it commits the program to regulatory filing. G5 includes the CEO because a submission carries the organization's reputation with the FDA. G6–G7 are Class III only. G6 involves the DSMB Chair for independent safety oversight; G7 is the broadest review team in the lifecycle.

Gate Principles

The OS enforces the sequence. No phase begins until the gate is passed. No gate is auto-approved. The system will not generate a V&V protocol until Design Freeze (G3) is passed, or compile an IDE until the Transfer Gate (G4) is passed. This prevents the most common failure mode: teams that start testing before the design is locked, or start writing submissions before testing is complete.

Every gate has the same structure. One week before, the OS publishes the complete evidence package. Each voter reviews independently (audio feedback is the default). On gate day, the H​Venture Lead presents the OS recommendation and the team votes: GO, CONDITIONAL GO, or NO-GO. The vote and all evidence are logged permanently.

9.2 Reimbursement Execution

Agents: MP MarketPilot + RN RegNavigator + DW DocuWriter

Section 3 builds the health economics case - why payers should cover the device. This section is about the execution: actually obtaining codes, securing coverage, and getting hospitals paid.

Current Industry Pain Points

  • Reimbursement strategy starts too late. Most companies begin payer engagement 6-12 months after clearance. By then, hospitals have already decided the device is "too expensive" based on incomplete economic analysis, and early adoption stalls.
  • CPT/HCPCS coding takes 12-24 months. New code applications to the AMA CPT Editorial Panel or CMS HCPCS process follow annual cycles with rigid submission deadlines. A missed deadline means a full year's delay. Companies that don't plan the coding pathway during development miss the first available window after clearance.
  • Coverage determination is fragmented. CMS national coverage determinations (NCDs) are rare for devices. Most coverage happens through local coverage determinations (LCDs) from Medicare Administrative Contractors (MACs), commercial payer policies, and hospital value analysis committees - each requiring a different evidence package and a different stakeholder conversation.
  • Hospital value analysis is a black box. Hospitals evaluate new devices through value analysis committees (VACs) that assess clinical need, cost impact, and operational workflow. These committees meet monthly, require specific documentation formats, and reject devices that don't demonstrate a clear total cost-of-care benefit. Companies submit generic dossiers and wait months for a decision.
  • Pediatric reimbursement has unique challenges. Pediatric procedures are lower-volume, often undercoded (bundled into adult DRGs), and disproportionately covered by Medicaid (which pays 30-50% less than commercial rates). The economic case must account for lower reimbursement levels and demonstrate value differently than adult devices.

The coverage strategy is matched to where the clinical study sites are located (they become the first adopters).
  • Evidence dossier generation: DW​DocuWriter generates payer-specific evidence dossiers from the program's health economics model, clinical data, and published literature.   • Pediatric device development costs 30-50% more than adult equivalents for a fraction of the market

2. Technical Approach - Quorum Architecture

2.1 What Quorum Is

Quorum is a coordinated system of 14 specialized AI agents - each trained on domain-specific medtech knowledge - that operate under human expert oversight at every decision point. The agents handle requirements interpretation, coordination, documentation, regulatory navigation, quality compliance, supply chain operations, IP creation, and financial intelligence. The humans handle judgment, invention, and clinical decision-making. The quality system becomes invisible to the engineer and visible to the auditor.

The key insight: medical device development is 70% coordination and documentation, 30% invention and judgment. The single largest productivity drag is requirements interpretation — engineers, quality specialists, and regulatory professionals spending weeks reading guidance documents, cross-correlating requirements, and mapping them to design inputs. Quorum automates the entire chain and amplifies the 30%.

Voice-first architecture: Every team member interacts with Quorum through voice — professional headphones with a microphone, instant transcription, and voice-commanded workflows from any location (bench, lab, phone, home). Documents are signed by voice command with Part 11–compliant electronic signatures. There is no typing, no form-filling, no context-switching. The result: dramatically faster feedback loops, zero documentation overhead, and a daily signing routine that replaces hours of weekly paperwork.

2.3 AI/ML Architecture

2.3.1 Foundation Model Strategy. Hybrid RAG + selective fine-tuning. Model-agnostic abstraction layer allows swapping underlying models as capabilities improve. No vendor lock-in.

2.3.2 Training Data Architecture. Three tiers:

  • Tier 1 - Regulatory & Standards (public): FDA guidance, 510(k)/PMA/De Novo database (37K+ records), MAUDE pediatric subset (~85K), ISO standards, EU MDR, Harvard-MIT CRS. ~4.2M documents, ~18B tokens.
  • Tier 2 - Institutional (BCH-restricted, de-identified): BCH CHIP EHR (30 years), surgical outcomes databases, clinical trial templates, prior FDA submissions. Governed by BCH IRB.
  • Tier 3 - Dynamic (real-time): FDA Federal Register (24-hour pipeline), new MAUDE reports (weekly), ISO revisions, PubMed monitoring, PCX updates.
  • Tier 4 - Studio Observational (new, unique to this approach): Real workflows observed from the live studio - design review transcripts, test report drafts, regulatory strategy documents, engineering decision rationale. This tier is what makes Quorum different from any academic AI project: it learns from practitioners doing real work, not from published literature alone.

2.3.3 Agent Orchestration. TaskPilot coordinates all agents via: DAG-based task graph engine (Temporal/Prefect), structured JSON-LD inter-agent communication, and Human-in-the-Loop gates (HOL-1 informational, HOL-2 review required, HOL-3 signature required per 21 CFR Part 11). There is no HOL-0 - no agent output is invisible to human oversight. Inter-agent conflicts are escalated to humans, never resolved autonomously. Every gate requires a quorum of qualified human decision-makers — the minimum number required to authorize advancement. The agents do the work; the quorum validates it.

2.3.4 Hallucination Mitigation. Four-layer validation: source grounding with inline citations, cross-agent verification for high-stakes outputs, automated consistency checks against regulatory databases, and human expert review for all HOL-2/3 outputs.

2.3.5 Novel Capabilities. Several capabilities are unique to Quorum and have no equivalent in existing medtech tools:

  • Automated requirements cross-correlation: RegNavigator reads all applicable FDA guidance, ISO standards, and predicate submissions for the device class, cross-correlates requirements to clinical needs, and generates the initial SRS and RTM — the 4–8 week interpretive work that currently requires senior engineers and regulatory specialists, done overnight.
  • Evidence-based risk occurrence rates: RiskGuard mines MAUDE complaint data, published literature, and cross-program failure history to generate evidence-based occurrence rates from real-world data — replacing the subjective engineer estimates that populate most DFMEAs.
  • SOUP tracking from Day 1: DesignCopilot catalogs every Software of Unknown Provenance component the moment it is introduced — version, license, known anomalies, risk class impact — maintaining a live SOUP list with V&V initiated on each component at download, not months later.
  • Supplier API integration: SupplyPilot connects directly to supplier systems for real-time inventory, lead times, and pricing — with auditor assistant chat for field inspections.
  • Cross-program compounding: Every CMO engagement, supplier qualification, patent prosecution, risk assessment, and cost benchmark feeds into shared studio intelligence. The 12th program launches with institutional knowledge from the first 11.

2.3.6 PRR-Bench. The Pediatric Regulatory Reasoning Benchmark - a published, standardized evaluation framework:

  • 50 historical pediatric 510(k) submissions with known outcomes
  • 25 De Novo and PMA submissions with pediatric indications
  • 100 pediatric MAUDE scenarios requiring risk classification
  • 30 clinical trial design scenarios requiring pediatric-specific methodology
  • Targets: pathway accuracy >95%; predicate precision/recall >90%/>85%; document quality ≥3.8/5.0 (scored by ≥3 blinded former CDRH reviewers, Cohen's kappa >0.75)

In a proof-of-concept (February 2026), the team used a commercial LLM (Claude, Anthropic) with prompt engineering and RAG against BCH's regulatory precedent database to generate a predicate analysis for a pediatric device program. Scored by a blinded former CDRH reviewer (15 years experience) at 3.7/5.0 ("acceptable with moderate revisions - comparable to a mid-level regulatory associate"). All Tier 2 training within BCH's HIPAA-compliant cloud enclave (Azure Health Data Services). No patient data leaves BCH. IRB protocol submitted Month 1. Immutable audit trail for all data access.

2.4 Platform Validation & FDA Compliance

Quorum is not a medical device - it does not diagnose or treat patients. But its QMS functions (electronic records, electronic signatures, DHF management, CAPA tracking, design history, supplier qualification) must be validated software before any studio program can rely on it for quality records that FDA will audit. This is the same regulatory bar that commercial eQMS platforms (Greenlight Guru, MasterControl, Veeva Vault QMS) must clear. Without it, the platform is a research prototype, not a deployable tool.

2.4.1 Regulatory Framework. Quorum's QMS modules must comply with:

  • 21 CFR Part 11: Electronic records and electronic signatures. Audit trails, access controls, signature manifestations, system validation documentation. Every quality record generated through Quorum must be legally equivalent to a signed paper record.
  • 21 CFR 820 (Quality System Regulation): The QMS functionality (document control, CAPA, DHF, design controls, supplier management) must produce records that satisfy FDA QSR requirements during facility audits.
  • ISO 13485:2016 §4.1.6: Validation of computer software used in the quality management system. Risk-based approach per GAMP 5 (Good Automated Manufacturing Practice).
  • EU MDR Annex IX: For future EU market access, the QMS must meet the notified body audit requirements for computerized systems.

2.4.2 Validation Approach. Computer System Validation (CSV) following GAMP 5 risk-based methodology:

  • System classification: Quorum's QMS modules are GAMP Category 5 (custom software). AI agent outputs that feed into quality records require additional validation specific to non-deterministic systems - a novel challenge the program will address and publish.
  • Validation deliverables: Validation Master Plan, User Requirements Specification (URS), Functional Requirements Specification (FRS), Design Specification (DS), Installation Qualification (IQ), Operational Qualification (OQ), Performance Qualification (PQ), Traceability Matrix, Validation Summary Report.
  • AI-specific validation: Standard CSV assumes deterministic software (same input → same output). Quorum's agents are non-deterministic. The program will develop and publish a validation framework for AI-assisted QMS functions, including: output consistency testing (statistical bounds on agent variation), accuracy benchmarking (PRR-Bench integration), drift detection (ongoing monitoring of agent output quality), and human-override documentation (every agent output that a human modifies is logged as a training signal).
  • Part 11 technical controls: Immutable audit trail (every record creation, modification, and signature logged with timestamp, user identity, and reason for change), role-based access control, electronic signature with two-factor authentication, system backup and disaster recovery, automatic session timeout.

2.4.3 Validation Timeline. CSV is not a one-time event - it phases with the platform development:

  • Phase 1 (Mo 1-18): Validation Master Plan authored. URS and FRS developed in parallel with agent development. Part 11 technical controls implemented in platform architecture from Day 1 (not retrofitted).
  • Phase 2 (Mo 18-30): IQ/OQ executed on QMS modules (document control, CAPA, DHF, electronic signatures). QualityGuard agent validated against manual QMS outputs from studio's traditional operations. Gap analysis vs. Greenlight Guru / MasterControl feature parity.
  • Phase 3 (Mo 30-42): PQ executed on live studio program. AI-specific validation framework published (novel contribution). Full Part 11 compliance audit by independent assessor. Platform cleared for production use on quality records.
  • Phase 4 (Mo 42-54): Continuous validation - ongoing monitoring of agent output quality, periodic re-qualification, change control for model updates. FDA establishment registration if required for QMS software distribution.

Novel Contribution: Validating Non-Deterministic AI in QMS

No published framework exists for validating AI/ML-powered quality management systems under 21 CFR Part 11 and ISO 13485. Current GAMP 5 guidance assumes deterministic software. Quorum's validation work will produce the first peer-reviewed methodology for CSV of agentic AI in regulated medical device quality systems - a contribution that benefits the entire industry, not just this program. This methodology will be published and included in the open-source release.

D.2 Platform Architecture and Key Capabilities
What Quorum Actually Does
Voice-First Interface

Engineers speak instead of type. Everything is transcribed and filed into the correct record. This matters especially in pediatric development: engineers work with miniaturized components under magnification, alongside clinical teams (surgeons, pediatricians, perfusionists) in shared children's hospital spaces. A 15-minute typed review takes less than a minute spoken. Documents signed by voice command (Part 11-compliant). Camera capture feeds photos of tiny prototypes directly into the live DHF.

Requirements Cross-Correlation

Worse for pediatrics than any other category: many devices have no adult predicate (novel congenital indications), and those that do must navigate FDA's pediatric extrapolation guidance with different physiology, failure modes, and risk tolerances. RN RegNavigator identifies pediatric-specific requirements (ICH E11, extrapolation framework, age-stratified biocompatibility), cross-correlates them, and generates the initial SRS and RTM overnight. 4-8 weeks of senior labor, done before the first design review.

Evidence-Based Risk Occurrence

A failure mode acceptable in an 80 kg adult can be lethal in a 3 kg neonate, yet published pediatric failure data is far thinner. RG RiskGuard mines 85,000+ pediatric MAUDE records and cross-program history to generate weight-adjusted occurrence rates. Each DFMEA hazard arrives pre-populated, flagging where adult risk tolerances do not translate (higher metabolic rates, growth changes, smaller anatomical margins).
